The idea in
one sentence
Commercial anatomy models cost hundreds of dollars and rarely reach under-resourced schools. We're building a suite of mechanical organ models — starting with the heart — that any school can 3D-print and assemble for around $30, using materials from a hardware store.
Design philosophy

Built to be understood, not just observed.

Every design decision starts with one question: can a student figure out how this works just by looking at and using it? No motors, no hidden mechanisms, no black boxes.

We're targeting schools that don't have access to expensive lab equipment — which means every model must be low-cost, replicable on a consumer 3D printer, and documented well enough that a teacher can build it without our help.

🤲 Hands-on by design

Students physically operate each model rather than passively observing. The heart model uses manual pistons — you feel the resistance, the suction, the pressure.

👁️ Visually transparent

Colored water flows through clear tubing. One-way valves are visible. Nothing is hidden. The goal is that the mechanics teach the biology.

💸 Radically affordable

Our heart model prototype costs around $30 in materials — all 3D-printable, all off-the-shelf. That's the target for every model we build.

📂 Open source

CAD files, build guides, and documentation will be freely available. We're building for access, not profit.

Starting with the heart.
Building toward the whole body.

The heart model is our first proof of concept — but ModAnatomy's vision is a full library of organ system models. Each one designed around the same principles: mechanical, transparent, affordable, open.

About ModAnatomy

The body is
mechanical.
Teach it that way.

We're an early-stage project building open-source anatomy teaching tools for classrooms that can't afford commercial alternatives.

The problem

Good anatomy tools exist. They're just expensive.

Commercial heart models — the ones with removable chambers and color-coded anatomy — cost $150 to $400. Full organ system labs are out of reach for most public school budgets. The result is that biology is often taught with flat diagrams and verbal descriptions, which works for some students and leaves others behind.

We think there's a better way: mechanical models that make the physics of biology visible and tangible, built from materials that any school can afford and any teacher can replicate.

Where we are

Early stage, but the core idea is proven.

Our heart model prototype (V1) successfully demonstrates suction, pressure, unidirectional flow, and manual actuation — all for around $30 in materials, with fully 3D-printable parts. We're now refining the design and exploring what comes next.

01

Accessible by default

We design with under-resourced schools as the primary user, not an afterthought. Cost and replicability are core constraints, not nice-to-haves.

02

Open everything

CAD files, build documentation, and assembly guides will be freely available. Our goal is that any school can replicate our models without us in the room.

03

Honest about what we know

We've proven the mechanical concept. We haven't yet run classroom pilots or measured learning outcomes — and we'll be clear about that distinction as we grow.

Long-term vision

A full library of organ models — one organ at a time.

The heart is first because the cardiac cycle has clear mechanical analogs: chambers, valves, pressure, flow. We believe similar approaches can work for lungs (bellows), kidneys (filtration), and more.

We're also interested in comparative anatomy — building 2-chamber fish hearts, 3-chamber amphibian hearts, and 4-chamber mammal hearts side by side to illustrate evolutionary biology.

The vision

One organ. Then another. Then the whole system.

We're not trying to build everything at once. The heart model comes first because it's mechanically tractable and educationally important. If it works well in classrooms, we'll use those lessons to design the next model.

The long-term goal is a modular library — organs that can be taught individually or connected into a system. All at the same price point. All open source.

How it works

The mechanics, step by step

01

Pull stroke — Diastole

Pulling the lever arm retracts the piston, creating a vacuum inside the chamber. Suction draws colored water in through the inlet check valve — a mechanical analog for how the heart's chambers fill with blood during diastole.

02

Push stroke — Systole

Pressing the lever arm drives the piston forward, building pressure. The outlet check valve opens and water is ejected into the tubing — analogous to the heart's systolic contraction.

03

Valve action — Unidirectional flow

Aquarium-style check valves prevent backflow at both inlet and outlet. Students can observe that colored water only travels in one direction — the same principle behind the heart's mitral, aortic, tricuspid, and pulmonary valves.

04

The key engineering challenge — Sealing a square piston

Creating a reliable vacuum with a square cross-section required several iterations. The solution: craft foam cut to the piston face (edges glued only, center left to flex), fully coated with 100% silicone caulk, and cured for 6–24 hours. The result is a compressible, waterproof gasket that seals under negative pressure — without needing circular geometry or O-rings.
