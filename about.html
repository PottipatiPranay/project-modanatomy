About Project ModAnatomy

The body is
mechanical.
Teach it that way.

We're an early-stage project building mechanical anatomy teaching tools and renting them to classrooms that can't afford commercial alternatives.

The problem

Good anatomy tools exist. They're just expensive.

Commercial heart models — the ones with removable chambers and color-coded anatomy — cost $150 to $400. Full organ system labs are out of reach for most public school budgets. The result is that biology is often taught with flat diagrams and verbal descriptions, which works for some students and leaves others behind.

We think there's a better way: mechanical models that make the physics of biology visible and tangible, rented to schools on a flexible basis so the barrier to access is a subscription, not a purchase.

Where we are

Early stage, but the core idea is proven.

Our heart model prototype (V1) successfully demonstrates suction, pressure, unidirectional flow, and manual actuation — all for a materials cost that makes a rental model viable. We're now refining the design and exploring what comes next.

01

Accessible by default

We design for under-resourced schools first. Renting sets rather than selling them keeps the cost of access low, and means we retain responsibility for quality and upkeep.

02

Hands-on learning

Students learn by doing. Every model is designed to be physically operated — not just observed — so the mechanics of the device teach the mechanics of the biology.

03

Honest about what we know

We've proven the mechanical concept. We haven't yet run classroom pilots or measured learning outcomes — and we'll be clear about that distinction as we grow.

Long-term vision

A full rental library of organ models — one organ at a time.

The heart is first because the cardiac cycle has clear mechanical analogs: chambers, valves, pressure, flow. We believe similar approaches can work for lungs, kidneys, and more — each available as a rentable set.

We're also interested in comparative anatomy — building 2-chamber fish hearts, 3-chamber amphibian hearts, and 4-chamber mammal hearts side by side to illustrate evolutionary biology.

The vision

One organ. Then another. Then the whole system.

We're not trying to build everything at once. The heart model comes first because it's mechanically tractable and educationally important. If it works well in classrooms, we'll use those lessons to design the next model.

The long-term goal is a modular rental library — organ sets that can be checked out individually or as a system, returned, maintained by us, and sent to the next school.

Hands-on anatomy education for every classroom.
